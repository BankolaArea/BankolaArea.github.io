Strategic Framework for Augmenting Domestic Coking Coal Production

1. Background & Rationale

India’s steel sector is heavily dependent on imported coking coal, despite holding over 18.83 billion tonnes of reserves. In FY 2022-23, India imported more than 85% of its coking coal requirement, primarily from Australia and the US.1 The growing demand for steel (target: 300 MT per year by 2030) and volatile global prices make self-reliance in coking coal crucial.2

2. Inter-Ministerial Committee (IMC) and the Kapila Report

The Ministry of Coal constituted an Inter-Ministerial Committee (IMC) in 2021 to develop a roadmap for increasing domestic coking coal supplies.3 This IMC (guided by industry experts such as Mr. Kapil Dhagat) produced a report now commonly referred to as the "Kapila Report", which underpins India's current strategy.3

Fact: “Raw coking coal production in the country is projected to rise from 45 MT in 2021 to 140 MT by 2030, per the IMC recommendations.” (NITI Aayog, p. 195)2

3. Strategic Pillars & Key Recommendations

3.1. Accelerated Exploration and Projectization

  • Expand Exploration: Expedite government-led regional and detailed exploration of coking coal blocks, especially in Jharia, West Bokaro, and North Karanpura coalfields.
    “Unexplored and partially explored reserves are to be brought into production” (Ministry of Coal, 2023, p. 39).4
  • Projectize Reserves: Address project delays due to fire, land, and law & order issues, especially in Jharia, where 5.33 BT reserves remain fire-affected.
    “Legacy issues in Jharia require a special Jharia Action Plan for rehabilitation and fire control.” (NITI Aayog, p. 197)2

3.2. Expanding and Upgrading Washery Capacity

  • New Washeries: CIL and its subsidiaries are tasked with building new washeries, raising the national washing capacity from 21 MT to 61 MT.
    “CIL to set up 9 new coking coal washeries by 2025...” (PIB, 2022)5
  • Modernization: Emphasis on technology to improve yield and reduce ash content in washed coal.
    “New washeries shall adopt the latest technology for ash reduction and yield maximization.” (NITI Aayog, p. 198)2

3.3. Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) and Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs)

  • SPVs: The report recommends forming SPVs between CIL and Integrated Steel Plants (ISPs), such as SAIL and Tata Steel, for joint mine development and washery operations.
    “Formation of SPVs to combine public-sector mining and private-sector efficiency...” (NITI Aayog, p. 199)2
Case: Tata Steel’s West Bokaro washery, an SPV, processes 6.5 MT/year of raw coal for low-ash coking coal supply.2

3.4. Auction and Commercial Mining Reforms

  • Liberalized Auctions: Auction more coking coal blocks to private sector under revenue-sharing and PPP terms, including partially explored blocks.
    “The methodology for auctioning partially explored blocks should be revised to encourage private investment.” (NITI Aayog, p. 198)2

3.5. Technology Adoption for Steel Blending

  • Stamp Charging: Encourage steel plants to adopt stamp-charging oven technology, which allows greater use of higher-ash indigenous coking coal in the blend.
    “Use of stamp-charging ovens can reduce washed coking coal demand and promote domestic use.” (NITI Aayog, p. 199)2

4. Implementation Highlights (2023–2024)

  • CIL has commissioned new washeries at Patherdih and Madhuband.
    “Washed coking coal production was 6.6 MT in FY 2022-23; two new washeries were commissioned.” (CIL Annual Report 2022-23, p. 49)6
  • More than 15 blocks have been auctioned to private sector for coking coal production.
    “Fifteen coking coal blocks allocated to private parties through auctions and revenue-sharing.” (PIB, 2023)7

5. Social & Environmental Concerns

  • DMF Funds: Mining auction proceeds are channeled to District Mineral Foundations for the benefit of affected communities.
    “DMF is now a key channel for sharing mining revenue with local communities.” (Down to Earth, 2022)8
  • Jharia Remediation: Special funds allocated for Jharia fire-affected area rehabilitation.
    “Jharia Action Plan envisions more than ₹10,000 crore for fire-fighting and rehabilitation.” (NITI Aayog, p. 197)2

6. Outlook

The “Mission Coking Coal” aims for 140 MT of domestic raw coking coal production by 2030, robust washery capacity, and reduced imports, but success depends on: expediting exploration, executing Jharia action plans, ensuring private sector interest, and sustained technology upgradation.2
