Historical Timeline of Coal Price Regulation in India

1945: Colliery Control Order Establishes Price Controls

The Colliery Control Order, 1945 was issued under the Defence of India Rules, empowering the Central Government to fix grade-wise and colliery-wise coal prices.
The Order continued as per the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, and the government retained the power to centrally determine coal prices for all collieries in India.
[2, 3]

1994: Last Fixed Price Revision by Government

The final comprehensive price revision under governmental control was announced on 17 June 1994, covering all grades and types of coal.
Some smaller amendments and adjustments followed in 1995 and 1996 for transport costs or specific mines/plants.
[3]

1996-1997: Partial Deregulation Begins

In March 1996, prices of coking coal and A, B, C grades of non-coking coal were deregulated based on the Bureau of Industrial Costs and Prices (BICP) recommendations.
March 1997 saw further deregulation, extending to soft coke, hard coke, and D-grade non-coking coal.
Prices of lower grades (E, F, G) remained under government oversight, with CIL and SCCL allowed to revise them semi-annually with government approval.
[3, 5]

2000: Complete Deregulation of Coal Prices

The Colliery Control Order, 2000 was notified and came into force on 1 January 2000.
It eliminated the government's power to fix coal prices, granting pricing autonomy to coal companies such as Coal India Limited (CIL) and Singareni Collieries Company Limited (SCCL).
[1, 2, 3]

2000s–Present: The NTPC Rate and Notified Prices for Power Sector

Even after deregulation, Coal India Limited (CIL) continued to set a “notified price” for coal supplied to major power utilities such as NTPC.
The NTPC rate, typically lower than prices for non-power industries or e-auction rates, ensures affordable coal for India's power sector.
This lower rate is cross-subsidized by higher industrial and e-auction prices.
As per the latest revision (Jan 2018), notified prices for the power sector remain substantially below prevailing e-auction and market rates.
[2], [3], [8]

2005: E-Auction System Introduced

CIL introduced an e-auction system in November 2004 (trial at BCCL), rolling it out nationwide in April 2005. This allowed market-based price discovery for coal sales outside the linkage system.
[4, 6]

2018–Present: National Coal Index & Market Pricing

From 2018, CIL moved toward dynamic, market-linked pricing. The National Coal Index (NCI) was launched in 2020 to track prices and guide auctions.
E-auction sales account for a significant share of CIL's revenue, often realizing premiums above notified prices.
[7]
